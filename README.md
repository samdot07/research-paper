# Hedging Under Pressure

**A Network-Portfolio Theory of ASEAN Strategic Adjustment to U.S.-China Competition, 2019–2025**

## Overview

This repository contains a research study that reconceptualises "hedging" — the strategy by which small and middle powers cultivate simultaneous ties to competing great powers rather than committing to either — as a process of **network-portfolio construction**. The framework combines two logics:

- **Network redundancy** (from resilience engineering): survival depends on maintaining multiple relational pathways to prevent catastrophic isolation.
- **Portfolio optimisation** (from financial theory): diversification reduces risk while preserving expected return.

Using this lens, the study traces how ASEAN hedging strategies evolved across five historical phases (1989–present) and disaggregates hedging into five components — economic pragmatism, binding engagement, limited bandwagoning, dominance denial, and indirect balancing — each with a distinct risk-return profile.

## Key Argument

Rather than uniform convergence, structurally similar ASEAN states have produced **three discrete hedging outcomes**:

| Outcome Type | Example | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Balanced Engagement** | Singapore | Symmetric economic, security, and diplomatic ties to both powers |
| **Asymmetric Security Tilt** | Vietnam | Deep China economic integration alongside intensified U.S. defense cooperation |
| **Volatile Oscillation** | Philippines | Dramatic alignment reversals across presidential administrations |

Malaysia, Indonesia, and Thailand occupy intermediate configurations, each reaching hedging stability through a distinct pathway (leverage cultivation, constitutional constraint, and strategic-culture compensation, respectively).

A central move of the study is repositioning **geographic proximity** from a deterministic cause to a *moderating* variable, whose effects depend on institutional capacity and leadership characteristics.

## Structure of the Study

1. **Introduction** — the puzzle of divergent hedging outcomes under comparable structural pressure
2. **Theoretical Framework** — hedging as dynamic network-portfolio construction; five temporal phases; five-component risk-return taxonomy; geographic proximity as moderator
3. **Configurational Logic & Methodology** — three outcome types, leadership typology, ASEAN's institutional role
4. **Six Case Studies** — Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia, Vietnam, the Philippines, Thailand
5. **Configurational Synthesis** — truth table of pathways, critical junctures (2019–2025), theoretical contributions, policy implications, scenario-based sustainability forecasting
6. **Conclusion** — summary findings and their bearing on the post-2025 strategic environment

## Core Findings

- **Territorial disputes, not mainland geography alone**, drive asymmetric security tilt (the Vietnam–Thailand contrast is the clearest evidence).
- **Institutional capacity matters through multiple, non-interchangeable channels**: bureaucratic professionalisation (Singapore, Vietnam), constitutional constraint (Indonesia), and strategic-culture compensation (Thailand).
- **Leadership effects are largest where institutions are weakest** — the Philippines' volatility and Singapore's consistency are mirror-image confirmations of the same interaction effect.
- Hedging's viability rests on three enabling conditions: absence of unambiguous existential threat, absence of ideological bloc division, and absence of unrestrained great-power rivalry — conditions increasingly strained by supply-chain competition, technology-standard bifurcation, and Taiwan Strait tensions.
